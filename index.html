🏛️ The Discovery

Tucked into the hills of North Canterbury, New Zealand, lies a limestone overhang that has witnessed centuries of history. The Weka Pass Historic Reserve was officially established in 1969 — but the stories written on its walls stretch back far longer.

🎨 Ancient Canvas

Māori were the first to call this place home — even if only briefly. They visited the area seasonally for mahinga kai (food gathering), hunting abundant birds including the now-extinct moa, as well as weka, kererū, and kākā. Under the shelter of the limestone overhang, they would rest by night — and leave their mark by day.

Using charcoal from their fires and red ochre (haematite), known as kōkōwai, they drew simple human figures, fish, and dogs on the limestone walls. Some were careful, deliberate works. Others — just as precious — were the carefree scribbles of daily life.

🔬 First Study

When European shepherds and farmers arrived, they too sheltered under the overhang — and inadvertently brought the drawings to wider attention. In 1876, Julius Haast, Director of Canterbury Museum, conducted the first scientific investigation. He commissioned artist T.S. Cousins to copy the main figures and excavated the shelter floor, unearthing flaked stone tools, charcoal, fire stones, and food remains. The evidence confirmed Māori occupation between 600–1,000 years ago.

⚠️ The Conservation Tragedy

In 1929, at the direction of W.R.B. Oliver, Director of the Dominion Museum, the original drawings were overpainted in red and black house paint to make them "more visible." At the time, this seemed like a reasonable act of preservation. In hindsight, it is regarded as a catastrophe — the original figures, painted centuries ago with natural pigments, were buried forever beneath modern house paint, destroying their scientific and historic value irreversibly.

🪨 Today

A fence now stands floor-to-ceiling between visitors and the drawings. The 40-minute walk across private farmland — closed during lambing from 1 August to 1 October — offers time to consider what we nearly lost. This 3D model captures what remains: the limestone overhang, the overpainted figures, and the weight of a story still being written.

"Māori at the limestone shelter, Weka Pass" — painted by Thomas Selby Cousins, commissioned by Julius Haast, Director of Canterbury Museum, 1876. The work captures a group of Māori before the natural limestone overhang, with drawings of canoes and fish visible beneath the shelter. A large proportion of South Island Māori rock art was produced on limestone shelters like this one.
